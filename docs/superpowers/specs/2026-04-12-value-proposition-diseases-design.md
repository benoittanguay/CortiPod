## Section 1: The Invisible Hormone Problem (Shared Intro)

**Hook:** Cortisol is the single most influential hormone in daily human functioning — it regulates energy, immunity, metabolism, sleep, mood, and stress response. Yet it is the least monitored.

**Key stats to weave in:**

- 76% of U.S. adults say stress impacts their health (APA 2025)
- Cortisol follows a diurnal curve that is clinically meaningful — a single lab test at 8 AM tells you almost nothing about what happens at 3 PM or 11 PM
- Dozens of conditions involve cortisol dysregulation, affecting tens of millions of North Americans
- Zero consumer devices measure cortisol directly today — every wearable infers stress from heart rate variability, an indirect proxy with significant noise

**Closing line:** "CortiPod is the first wrist-worn device that measures cortisol directly from sweat — making the invisible visible, continuously."

## Section 2: Investor Section — Clinical Conditions & Market Sizing

### Condition 1: Cushing's Syndrome / Cushing's Disease

**Description:** Excess cortisol production. Causes weight gain, muscle wasting, hypertension, diabetes, osteoporosis, psychiatric symptoms. Often takes 3-6 years to diagnose because symptoms mimic common conditions.

**Prevalence:**
- NA: ~20,000-26,000 diagnosed cases in the US; estimated 57-79 per million population
- Global: incidence of 1.8-4.5 per million/year; recent Wisconsin data suggests true incidence may be 2-3x higher (7-15 per million/year)

**Current monitoring:** Single morning blood cortisol, 24-hour urine free cortisol, late-night salivary cortisol, dexamethasone suppression test — all snapshots, often requiring multiple rounds to confirm.

**CortiPod value:** Continuous diurnal curve reveals the loss of normal cortisol rhythm (the hallmark of Cushing's) in a way that single-point tests cannot. Could dramatically shorten the diagnostic odyssey.

**Sources:**

### Condition 2: Addison's Disease (Primary Adrenal Insufficiency)

**Description:** The adrenal glands don't produce enough cortisol. Life-threatening if untreated. Symptoms are vague — fatigue, weight loss, low blood pressure, salt craving — making it notoriously hard to diagnose until an adrenal crisis occurs.

**Prevalence:**
- NA: ~40-60 per million (~13,000-20,000 in the US)
- Global: 1 in 20,000 persons in Western countries

**Current monitoring:** Morning serum cortisol + ACTH stimulation test. Once diagnosed, patients are on lifelong cortisol replacement (hydrocortisone) but have no way to verify their dosing produces a normal cortisol curve throughout the day.

**CortiPod value:** For diagnosed patients on replacement therapy, continuous monitoring could optimize dosing timing and detect under/over-replacement. For undiagnosed patients, a persistently flat cortisol curve could prompt earlier medical evaluation.

**Sources:**
- [Addison Disease — StatPearls/NIH](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK441994/)
- [Primary Adrenal Insufficiency — Merck Manual](https://www.merckmanuals.com/professional/endocrine-and-metabolic-disorders/adrenal-disorders/primary-adrenal-insufficiency-addison-disease)
- [Addison's Disease — Cleveland Clinic](https://my.clevelandclinic.org/health/diseases/15095-addisons-disease)
- [Definition & Facts — NIDDK](https://www.niddk.nih.gov/health-information/endocrine-diseases/adrenal-insufficiency-addisons-disease/definition-facts)

### Condition 3: Secondary Adrenal Insufficiency (Steroid-Induced)

**Description:** The most common form of adrenal insufficiency — caused by long-term corticosteroid use suppressing the HPA axis. When steroids are tapered or stopped, the adrenals can't restart fast enough. Recovery takes 6-12 months and is unpredictable.

**Prevalence:**
- NA: ~2.5% of the US population is on chronic corticosteroids (~8 million people). Of those, 37-49% develop some degree of adrenal suppression — roughly 3-4 million people in the US alone.
- Global: proportional; corticosteroid use is widespread in all developed nations

**Current monitoring:** Periodic ACTH stimulation tests during taper — invasive, infrequent, done in clinic. No way to monitor recovery at home.

**CortiPod value:** Patients tapering off steroids could track their adrenal recovery in real time. The return of a normal diurnal cortisol curve would signal safe taper progression. This is the largest single clinical population in the document.

**Sources:**
- [Glucocorticoid-induced adrenal insufficiency — CCJM](https://www.ccjm.org/content/91/4/245)
- [Adrenal Insufficiency — StatPearls/NIH](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK441832/)
- [Recovery of steroid induced adrenal insufficiency — PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5682381/)
- [Adrenal Suppression — Endotext/NIH](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK279047/)

### Condition 4: Congenital Adrenal Hyperplasia (CAH)

**Description:** Genetic disorder affecting cortisol synthesis. The classic form is detected at birth via newborn screening. The non-classic form (NCCAH) is far more common and often undiagnosed — presents as early puberty, acne, hirsutism, irregular periods, and infertility.

**Prevalence:**
- NA: classic CAH ~1 in 15,000 births (~22,000 living with it in the US); non-classic CAH ~1 in 200-1,000 depending on ethnicity — potentially hundreds of thousands undiagnosed
- Global: classic ~1:15,000; non-classic ~1:1,000 births

**Current monitoring:** Serum 17-hydroxyprogesterone and cortisol blood draws, repeated periodically. Children with classic CAH require lifelong glucocorticoid replacement with frequent dose adjustments during growth.

**CortiPod value:** For CAH patients on replacement therapy (especially children/adolescents), continuous cortisol tracking could help parents and endocrinologists optimize dosing without repeated blood draws.

**Sources:**
- [Congenital Adrenal Hyperplasia — Medscape](https://emedicine.medscape.com/article/919218-overview)
- [CAH Epidemiology Forecast — DelveInsight](https://www.delveinsight.com/report-store/congenital-adrenal-hyperplasia-cah-epidemiology-forecast)
- [Ethnic and National Differences in CAH — Karger](https://karger.com/hrp/article/96/3/249/836387/Ethnic-and-National-Differences-in-Congenital)
- [Congenital Adrenal Hyperplasia — StatPearls/NIH](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK448098/)

### Condition 5: Chronic Stress & Burnout

**Description:** Not a single disease but a physiological state: prolonged HPA axis activation leading to cortisol dysregulation. Manifests as flattened diurnal curve, elevated evening cortisol, or blunted cortisol awakening response. Precursor to depression, cardiovascular disease, metabolic syndrome, and immune dysfunction.

**Prevalence:**
- NA: 76% of US adults report stress affects their health; 55% of the US workforce is experiencing burnout (2025); 72% face moderate-to-very-high work stress. Stress-related healthcare expenses totaled $190 billion in 2025; chronic job stress contributes to ~120,000 deaths/year in the US.
- Global: WHO estimates stress-related conditions cost the global economy $1 trillion/year in lost productivity

**Current monitoring:** None. No standard clinical tool for measuring chronic stress objectively. HRV is a proxy. Salivary cortisol tests exist but are single-point and impractical for daily tracking.

**CortiPod value:** The primary consumer use case. Makes stress visible and measurable. Users can see whether their cortisol follows a healthy curve, track the impact of lifestyle interventions, and catch dysregulation before it becomes a clinical problem.

**Sources:**
- [Workplace Burnout 2025 Report — The Interview Guys](https://blog.theinterviewguys.com/workplace-burnout-in-2025-research-report/)
- [Work-Related Stress 2025 — Wellhub](https://wellhub.com/en-us/blog/wellness-and-benefits-programs/work-related-stress-in-the-united-states/)
- [Stress Statistics 2026 — SingleCare](https://www.singlecare.com/blog/news/stress-statistics/)
- [APA Stress in America 2025](https://www.apa.org/pubs/reports/stress-in-america/2025)

### Condition 6: HPA Axis Dysfunction (Cortisol Rhythm Disorders)

**Description:** An umbrella for conditions where the hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal feedback loop is disrupted — cortisol is produced at the wrong times, in the wrong amounts, or doesn't respond normally to stress. Overlaps with depression (40-60% of depressed patients show HPA dysregulation), PTSD (7-12% lifetime prevalence), anxiety disorders, and chronic fatigue.

**Prevalence:**
- NA: ~21 million US adults have major depression and 40-60% of them show cortisol dysregulation — 8-13 million with depression-related HPA dysfunction alone. Add PTSD (~13 million), anxiety (~40 million), and the overlap is enormous.
- Global: depression affects ~280 million people worldwide (WHO)

**Current monitoring:** Cortisol is rarely measured in psychiatric care despite strong research evidence linking it to outcomes. When measured, it's a single morning blood draw.

**CortiPod value:** Could become a biomarker companion to mental health treatment. Tracking whether cortisol rhythm normalizes during therapy or medication could give clinicians and patients an objective signal of progress — something psychiatric care almost entirely lacks today.

**Sources:**
- [HPA Axis in Major Depression — PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5313380/)
- [HPA axis function in PTSD — PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6582238/)
- [Chronic Stress and Autoimmunity: HPA Axis — MDPI](https://www.mdpi.com/1422-0067/26/20/9994)
- [Integrative Approach to HPA Axis Dysfunction — ScienceDirect](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0002934325003535)

### Condition 7: Shift Work & Circadian Disruption

**Description:** Night shifts, rotating schedules, and chronic jet lag disrupt the cortisol circadian rhythm. Linked to increased risk of cardiovascular disease, type 2 diabetes, obesity, depression, and cancer (IARC classifies night shift work as a probable carcinogen).

**Prevalence:**
- NA: ~16 million Americans work non-daytime shifts (BLS). Approximately 10-40% of shift workers develop Shift Work Disorder.
- Global: ~20% of the global workforce does shift work

**Current monitoring:** None specific to cortisol. Sleep studies and actigraphy are used but don't measure the hormonal consequence of circadian disruption.

**CortiPod value:** Shift workers could see exactly how their cortisol rhythm is affected by their schedule and track whether interventions (light therapy, timed sleep, melatonin) are helping restore a healthier pattern.

## Section 3: Consumer Section — "Is This You?"

Each condition reframed as a symptom-based persona. No disease names in headers. Second person, warm but direct. Wellness-compliant framing throughout.

### Persona 1: "You've been gaining weight and no one can tell you why"

You're eating the same, exercising the same, but your body is changing — weight around your midsection and face, skin that bruises easily, muscles that feel weak. You've seen your doctor, maybe more than once. Blood work comes back "normal." You feel like something is wrong but can't prove it.

*What might be happening:* Your body may be producing too much cortisol — and a single morning blood test can miss it entirely. Cortisol problems often hide behind normal-looking lab results because they depend on timing.

*How CortiPod helps:* Track your cortisol curve across the full day. If your levels stay elevated when they should be dropping, you'll have real data to bring to your doctor — not just symptoms they can't see.

### Persona 2: "You're exhausted no matter how much you rest"

Deep, unshakeable fatigue. Low blood pressure. Craving salt. You push through but crash hard. Some days you can barely function, and the next day you feel almost fine. Doctors run thyroid panels and iron tests — everything's normal.

*What might be happening:* Your adrenal glands may not be producing enough cortisol. This is rare but real, and it's one of the most under-diagnosed conditions in medicine because the symptoms overlap with so many other things.

*How CortiPod helps:* A healthy cortisol curve peaks in the morning and tapers through the day. If yours is flat — low all day — that's a pattern worth showing your doctor. Continuous data reveals what a single blood draw at 8 AM cannot.

### Persona 3: "You're tapering off steroids and don't know if your body is recovering"

You've been on prednisone or another corticosteroid for months — maybe for asthma, arthritis, an autoimmune condition, or a transplant. Now your doctor is tapering the dose, and you feel terrible. Fatigue, joint pain, nausea. You don't know if it's your original condition coming back or your body struggling to restart its own cortisol production.

*What might be happening:* Long-term steroid use suppresses your adrenal glands. When you taper, they need time to wake back up — sometimes 6 to 12 months. There's currently no way to monitor this at home.

*How CortiPod helps:* Watch your natural cortisol production return in real time. As your body recovers, you'll see the morning peak start to reappear. Share the trend with your doctor to guide taper speed — faster when recovery is strong, slower when it's not.

### Persona 4: "Your child's medication dose never seems quite right"

Your child has a condition that requires daily cortisol replacement. Every few months, you're back at the endocrinologist for blood draws. Between visits, you're guessing — are they getting enough? Too much? Growth, energy, and mood all seem to fluctuate, and you don't have a way to track what's happening between appointments.

*What might be happening:* Children on cortisol replacement need doses adjusted as they grow. Blood draws are infrequent snapshots that can miss what's happening on a Tuesday afternoon.

*How CortiPod helps:* Continuous tracking between clinic visits gives you and your child's doctor a complete picture — not just one morning data point every three months. You can see whether the daily curve looks healthy, and flag concerns before the next appointment.

### Persona 5: "You feel like you're running on empty and burning out"

You know you're stressed — everyone tells you to slow down. But you don't know *how* stressed, or whether it's getting worse. You've tried meditation apps, breathwork, better sleep habits. Some seem to help, some don't. You're guessing.

*What might be happening:* Chronic stress changes your cortisol pattern. Instead of a healthy peak in the morning and a taper through the day, your curve may be flat, elevated at night, or barely rising in the morning. You can't feel this happening — it's invisible until it manifests as insomnia, weight gain, brain fog, or illness.

*How CortiPod helps:* See your stress hormone in real numbers, not just a mood score derived from your heart rate. Track whether your morning cortisol is healthy, whether it's dropping by evening, and whether the changes you're making are actually working. Data, not guesswork.

### Persona 6: "You're in treatment for depression or anxiety, but can't tell if it's working"

You started therapy, or medication, or both. Your therapist asks how you're feeling. You say "a little better, maybe?" There's no objective signal — no blood pressure cuff for mental health. Progress is invisible.

*What might be happening:* Up to 60% of people with depression have a measurably disrupted cortisol rhythm. As treatment works, that rhythm often normalizes — but no one is tracking it.

*How CortiPod helps:* An objective biomarker alongside your subjective experience. If your cortisol curve is normalizing over weeks, that's a real signal that something is shifting — even before you fully feel it. Something concrete to discuss with your clinician.

### Persona 7: "You work nights and feel like your body never adjusts"

Night shifts, rotating schedules, red-eye flights. You sleep during the day but never feel rested. Your energy is unpredictable. You've read that shift work is bad for your health, but you don't know what it's actually doing to your body.

*What might be happening:* Your cortisol rhythm is inverted or flattened. Your body is producing the wake-up hormone when you're trying to sleep and not enough when you need to be alert. Over time, this is linked to serious health consequences.

*How CortiPod helps:* See exactly how your schedule is affecting your cortisol cycle. Track whether strategies like timed light exposure, strategic napping, or meal timing are helping your body adapt. Your shift may not change, but how your body handles it can.

## Section 4: The CortiPod Advantage (Shared Closing)

Three differentiators:

1. **Direct measurement, not a proxy.** Every wearable on the market today infers stress from heart rate variability. CortiPod measures cortisol itself — the actual hormone — from sweat on your wrist. The difference is like estimating the weather from how people are dressed versus reading a thermometer.

2. **A curve, not a snapshot.** A single cortisol lab test is a photograph. CortiPod gives you the full movie — readings every 15 minutes, all day. Cortisol disorders are pattern disorders. You need the pattern to see them.

3. **On your wrist, not in a lab.** No blood draws. No 24-hour urine jugs. No saliva kits you have to mail away and wait two weeks for results. Wear it like a fitness band, see your data on your phone.

**Investor line:** "CortiPod addresses a $1B+ market across clinical and consumer segments — from rare endocrine disorders where early detection saves lives, to the 76% of Americans who say stress is harming their health and have no way to measure it."

**Consumer line:** "Your body has been telling you something. CortiPod lets you hear it."

# Brand Naming Frameworks for Biotech / Health-Tech / Wearables

Research synthesis from professional naming agencies and linguistic research.

---

## 1. Name Type Taxonomies

Professional agencies classify names along two axes: **approach** (what the name communicates) and **construct** (how the name is built).

### Approach Spectrum (Descriptive --> Abstract)

| Approach | Definition | Health-Tech Examples |
|---|---|---|
| **Descriptive** | Directly describes function, founder, or origin | 23andMe (23 chromosome pairs), Fitbit (fitness + bit of data), Dexcom (dextrose + com) |
| **Suggestive / Evocative** | Hints at brand qualities without stating them | Levels (suggests optimization tiers), Illumina (illumination/insight), Moderna (modern + RNA) |
| **Abstract / Arbitrary** | No inherent meaning; blank vessel filled by marketing | Oura (Finnish origin, sounds like "aura"), Whoop (exclamation repurposed), Garmin (founder names: Gary + Min) |

### Construct Types

| Construct | Definition | Examples |
|---|---|---|
| **Real word** | Existing dictionary word | Apple, Whoop, Levels, Grail, Tempus, Vertex |
| **Compound** | Two real words combined | Fitbit, BioNTech, Ultrahuman |
| **Coined** | Invented, truncated, or blended | Theranos (therapy + diagnosis), Moderna (modified + RNA), Illumina |

### Igor's Four Categories + Engagement Scale

Igor International uses a 4-category system with an engagement axis (-2 to +5):

1. **Functional** (-2 to 0 engagement): Named after people or describes what the company does. *Biogen, Amgen, Curagen.* Low memorability, quickly forgotten.
2. **Invented** (0 to +3): Made-up or non-English words. *Agilent, Google.* Can be powerful if phonetically appealing.
3. **Experiential** (+1 to +3): Direct connection to human experience. *Palm Pilot, Netscape.* Literal but presented with imagination.
4. **Evocative** (+3 to +5): Evokes positioning rather than function. *Apple, Virgin, Cracker Jack.* Highest engagement -- gives the audience the most material to "play with and personalize."

**Key insight**: Most biotech names cluster in the Functional or low-Experiential zone, which means standing out requires moving toward Evocative or high-Invented territory.

### Lexicon Branding's Diamond Framework

Before naming, answer four questions:
1. What does winning look like?
2. What must you win?
3. What's needed to win?
4. What needs to be communicated?

Then: generate 1,000+ ideas, narrow to 50-100 candidates, test through linguistic networks and consumer panels.

## 2. What Makes Biotech / Health-Tech Names Work

### Analysis of Successful Names

| Name | Type | Why It Works |
|---|---|---|
| **Whoop** | Real word, abstract | Visceral, energetic, one syllable. Originally a pre-game motivational shout. Conveys peak readiness. Impossible to forget. |
| **Oura** | Coined (Finnish) | Sounds like "aura" -- suggests invisible insight, holistic wellness. Soft vowels convey approachability. 4 letters. |
| **Dexcom** | Compound, descriptive | "Dextrose" + "com" (communication/company). Signals glucose expertise. Short, clinical, trustworthy. |
| **Libre** (Abbott) | Real word, evocative | French/Spanish for "free." Evokes liberation from fingerstick testing. Emotional positioning via a single word. |
| **Levels** | Real word, suggestive | Suggests both glucose levels (literal) and leveling up (aspirational). Clean, modern, dual meaning. |
| **Theranos** | Coined blend | "Therapy" + "diagnosis." Phonetically elegant. Greek roots convey scientific authority. (Name was excellent; company was fraud.) |
| **Illumina** | Coined, evocative | Latin "illuminare" = to light up. Suggests insight, revelation, clarity. Feminine ending (-a) feels approachable. |
| **Moderna** | Coined blend | "Modified" + "RNA." Signals modernity and innovation. The -a ending softens what could feel clinical. |
| **23andMe** | Compound, descriptive | 23 chromosome pairs + personal pronoun. Makes genomics feel personal and accessible. Conversational. |
| **Tempus** | Real word (Latin) | Latin for "time." Suggests urgency and timeliness of precision medicine. Classical authority. |
| **Grail** | Real word, evocative | Holy Grail = the ultimate quest. For a cancer detection company, it positions early detection as the holy grail of oncology. Maximum evocative power. |
| **Fitbit** | Compound, suggestive | "Fitness" + "bit" (data). Perfectly signals quantified fitness. Now feels generic (a risk of being *too* descriptive). |
| **Garmin** | Coined (founders) | Portmanteau of founders Gary Burrell and Min Kao. Became its own word through brand equity. |

### Patterns That Emerge

- **1-2 syllable real words dominate wearables**: Whoop, Oura, Muse, Cove, Cala, Levels
- **Latin/Greek roots dominate biotech**: Illumina, Moderna, Tempus, Theranos, Grail
- **The best names work on two levels**: Literal meaning + aspirational meaning (Levels, Libre, Grail)
- **Short names are trending**: The wearable space heavily favors 4-6 letter names
- **Vowel-ending names feel approachable**: Oura, Moderna, Illumina -- the terminal -a is consistently used in biotech/health

## 3. Phonetic and Linguistic Principles

### Sound Symbolism Research

Specific sounds carry unconscious associations that affect brand perception:

#### Vowels

| Vowel Type | Sounds Like | Perceived As | Best For |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Front vowels** (ee, ih, eh, ay) | Fitbit, Levels, Whoop | Smaller, lighter, faster, sharper, more precise | Precision, speed, tech, wearables |
| **Back vowels** (oo, oh, aw, uh) | Oura, Whoop, Moderna | Larger, heavier, more powerful, warmer | Authority, comfort, reliability |

#### Consonants

| Sound Type | Examples | Perceived As | Best For |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Plosives** (p, t, k, b, d, g) | Tempus, Grail, Fitbit | Strong, decisive, hard-edged, powerful | Precision instruments, clinical authority |
| **Fricatives** (f, s, v, z, sh) | Vertex, Azure | Fast, sleek, cutting-edge, lighter | Innovation, speed, modernity |
| **Sonorants** (m, n, l, r) | Illumina, Moderna, Oura | Warm, trustworthy, smooth, approachable | Wellness, consumer health, comfort |
| **Hard K/X sounds** | Dexcom, Lexicon, Vertex | Scientific precision, exactness | Clinical/diagnostic brands |

#### Key Principles

1. **Plosive-initial names get higher recognition**: Brands starting with k, p, t, b, d, g are remembered more easily (research: Oxford University)
2. **The bouba/kiki effect**: Rounded sounds (b, m, o, u) feel soft/organic; angular sounds (k, t, i, e) feel sharp/technical. Match your sound profile to your brand personality.
3. **Alliteration boosts recall**: PayPal, Kit-Kat, Coca-Cola, Fitbit
4. **Names ending in vowels feel more approachable** without losing authority: Moderna, Illumina, Oura
5. **Crisp consonant + short vowel patterns convey precision**: Think Vertex, Tempus, Grail

#### Sound Profile Recommendations for a Cortisol Wearable

- **If positioning as clinical/precision**: Favor plosives (k, t, p) and front vowels (eh, ih). Think: crisp, exact, scientific.
- **If positioning as wellness/consumer**: Favor sonorants (m, n, l) and back vowels (oh, oo, ah). Think: warm, approachable, holistic.
- **If positioning as innovative/disruptive**: Favor fricatives (s, v, z) and coined words. Think: new, cutting-edge, fast.
- **Hybrid approach** (clinical credibility + consumer warmth): Start with a plosive or fricative, end with a sonorant or vowel. Examples of this pattern: Tempus, Moderna, Grail.

## 4. Common Naming Mistakes in Health-Tech

### The Seven Deadly Mistakes

1. **Naming after your lead product/indication**: "CortisolSense" locks you into one analyte. When you expand to glucose, lactate, or multi-analyte, the name becomes a liability. Name for the platform, not the program.

2. **Over-reliance on Greek/Latin prefixes**: Bio-, Gen-, -omics, -tech suffixes are so overused in biotech that they create zero differentiation. Biogen, Curagen, Paradigm, Aradigm -- they all blur together.

3. **Misspellings for domain availability**: Dropping vowels (Flickr-style) or swapping letters signals "we couldn't get the real domain." It fails the SCRATCH test (Spelling Challenged).

4. **Insider jargon (Curse of Knowledge)**: Names that only make sense to your R&D team alienate investors, patients, and consumers. If you need to explain it, it's not working.

5. **Choosing a name that's too narrow/restrictive**: Location-based, single-disease, or single-technology names limit future growth and force expensive rebrands.

6. **Skipping trademark search**: A quick Google search is not a trademark search. Disputes cost $120K-$750K. Full rebrands cost $90K-$180K for small companies.

7. **Ignoring cross-language implications**: Test in French (critical for Quebec), Spanish, Mandarin, Arabic at minimum. Nokia's "Lumia" means "prostitute" in Spanish. Mazda's "Laputa" had the same problem.

## 5. The SMILE and SCRATCH Tests

From Alexandra Watkins (*Hello, My Name Is Awesome*):

### SMILE: Five Qualities of a Sticky Name

| Letter | Quality | Definition | Health-Tech Application |
|---|---|---|---|
| **S** | Suggestive | Evokes something about the brand | "Grail" suggests the ultimate diagnostic quest |
| **M** | Meaningful | Resonates with the *audience* (not just the creator) | "Libre" means freedom to the patient, not just the engineer |
| **I** | Imagery | Visually evocative, aids memory | "Illumina" conjures light/insight |
| **L** | Legs | Lends itself to themes, campaigns, extensions | "Levels" enables "level up," "next level," etc. |
| **E** | Emotional | Moves people | "Whoop" triggers excitement and energy |

### SCRATCH: Seven Deadly Sins of Naming

| Letter | Sin | Definition | Health-Tech Pitfall |
|---|---|---|---|
| **S** | Spelling Challenged | Looks like a typo | Lyft, Tumblr -- works for social apps, risky for health/trust |
| **C** | Copycat | Too similar to competitors | Another "Bio-" or "-gen" name |
| **R** | Restrictive | Limits future growth | "CortisolTrack" can't expand to multi-analyte |
| **A** | Annoying | Forced, try-hard, or frustrating | Overly clever portmanteaus that nobody gets |
| **T** | Tame | Flat, descriptive, uninspired | "Health Monitor Pro" |
| **C** | Curse of Knowledge | Only insiders understand it | "MIP-EIS Diagnostics" |
| **H** | Hard to Pronounce | Not obvious on first read | Anything requiring a pronunciation guide |

## 6. Name Architecture: How the Name Works Across Products

### Three Architecture Models

| Model | How It Works | Health-Tech Example | Best When |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Branded House (Monolithic)** | Everything under one master brand | Moderna (all products = Moderna), Exact Sciences, Illumina | Single-platform company, building brand equity in one name |
| **Endorsed Brands** | Sub-brands with parent brand endorsement | Roche > Genentech ("a member of the Roche Group") | Acquiring companies with existing equity |
| **House of Brands** | Independent brands, parent hidden | J&J > Band-Aid, Tylenol, Janssen | Diverse consumer products, risk isolation |

### For a Startup Wearable Company

**Branded House is almost always correct** for an early-stage wearable/diagnostics company:

- **Company name = Product name = App name**: Whoop (band) + Whoop (app). Oura (ring) + Oura (app). Levels (CGM) + Levels (app).
- **One name, one equity pool**: Every dollar of marketing builds the same brand
- **Sub-brand only when needed**: Dexcom G7 (product generation), Oura Ring Gen 3 (hardware version)
- **The app should share the company name**: Users search for it by brand. Mismatch creates friction.

### Name Extension Checklist

When evaluating a name candidate, test it in these contexts:

- [ ] "I wear my ____" (product reference)
- [ ] "Check your ____ app" (app reference)
- [ ] "____'s latest study shows..." (credibility/PR)
- [ ] "____ for Clinicians" (B2B sub-brand)
- [ ] "____ Labs" or "____ Research" (R&D arm)
- [ ] "Powered by ____" (platform/API licensing)
- [ ] As a verb: "I ____ed my levels this morning" (cultural penetration)

## 7. Actionable Evaluation Scorecard

Rate each name candidate 1-5 on these criteria:

| Criterion | Weight | Question |
|---|---|---|
| **Distinctiveness** | 15% | Does it stand apart from competitors in the space? |
| **Phonetic appeal** | 15% | Does it sound good spoken aloud? Is the sound profile right for the positioning? |
| **Memorability** | 15% | Can someone recall it after hearing it once? |
| **Expandability** | 10% | Does it work beyond cortisol, beyond wearables? |
| **Emotional resonance** | 10% | Does it make someone feel something? |
| **Pronounceability** | 10% | Obvious pronunciation in English and French? |
| **Spellability** | 5% | Can someone spell it after hearing it? |
| **Domain availability** | 5% | Is .com available or acquirable? |
| **Trademark clearance** | 10% | Clear in health/tech classes? |
| **Visual/logo potential** | 5% | Does the word have strong visual identity potential? |

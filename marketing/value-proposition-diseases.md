## The Invisible Hormone Problem

Cortisol is the single most influential hormone in daily human functioning. It regulates when you wake up and when you wind down. It controls how you respond to stress, how you store fat, how your immune system fights infection, and how your brain forms memories. Every system in your body listens to cortisol.

Yet it is the least monitored hormone in medicine.

Measuring cortisol today requires a blood draw, a saliva kit mailed to a lab, or a 24-hour urine collection. Each produces a single snapshot — one data point from one moment. But cortisol is not a static number. It follows a steep curve every day: surging in the morning, declining through the afternoon, reaching its lowest point at night. A single measurement at 8 AM tells you almost nothing about what happens at 3 PM, or whether your body is doing the right thing at midnight.

This matters because cortisol disorders are pattern disorders. The question is rarely "is your cortisol high?" — it's "is your cortisol high at the wrong time, for too long, or not rising when it should?" A single lab test cannot answer that question. Only a continuous curve can.

The need is enormous:

- **76% of U.S. adults** say stress is harming their health (APA, 2025)
- **Tens of millions** of North Americans live with conditions where cortisol is the primary biomarker — from rare endocrine diseases to chronic stress and burnout
- **Zero consumer devices** measure cortisol directly today. Every wearable on the market — Whoop, Oura, Apple Watch, Garmin — infers stress from heart rate variability, an indirect proxy with significant noise

CortiPod is the first wrist-worn device that measures cortisol directly from sweat — making the invisible visible, continuously.

## Clinical Conditions & Market Sizing

> **Note:** This section uses clinical language and disease names for investor and internal strategy purposes. It is not intended for consumer-facing marketing.

---

### 1. Cushing's Syndrome / Cushing's Disease

**What it is:** The body produces too much cortisol, either from an adrenal or pituitary tumor, or from prolonged corticosteroid medication. The excess cortisol causes progressive weight gain concentrated in the face and abdomen, muscle wasting, thinning skin that bruises easily, hypertension, type 2 diabetes, osteoporosis, and psychiatric symptoms including depression and cognitive impairment.

**Why it's hard to catch:** Cushing's is one of the most under-diagnosed conditions in endocrinology. The average time from first symptoms to diagnosis is 3-6 years because the individual symptoms — weight gain, fatigue, high blood pressure, mood changes — each look like something more common. Patients are often told they are simply stressed, eating too much, or aging.

**Prevalence:**
- North America: ~20,000-26,000 diagnosed cases in the US; estimated 57-79 per million population
- Global: incidence of 1.8-4.5 per million per year, though recent data from the University of Wisconsin suggests the true incidence may be 2-3x higher at 7-15 per million per year
- Many endocrinologists believe Cushing's is significantly under-diagnosed, particularly mild and cyclic forms

**Current monitoring gap:** Diagnosis relies on single-point tests — morning serum cortisol, 24-hour urine free cortisol, late-night salivary cortisol, and the dexamethasone suppression test. Each is a snapshot. Cyclic Cushing's, where cortisol surges intermittently, can evade all of them. Patients often undergo months of repeated testing before confirmation.

**CortiPod value:** A continuous diurnal cortisol curve would reveal the hallmark of Cushing's — the loss of normal rhythm, with cortisol remaining elevated when it should be low — in a way that single-point tests cannot. For cyclic Cushing's, continuous monitoring would catch the intermittent surges that scheduled lab visits miss. Could dramatically shorten the diagnostic odyssey from years to weeks.

### 2. Addison's Disease (Primary Adrenal Insufficiency)

**What it is:** The adrenal glands are damaged (usually by autoimmune attack) and cannot produce sufficient cortisol. Without replacement therapy, Addison's is fatal. Even with treatment, patients face chronic fatigue, dangerously low blood pressure, salt craving, weight loss, and the constant risk of adrenal crisis — a life-threatening emergency triggered by illness, injury, or stress.

**Why it's hard to catch:** Symptoms are vague and overlap with dozens of other conditions. Fatigue, weight loss, nausea, and low blood pressure are common complaints in primary care. Addison's is often not considered until a patient presents in crisis — hypotensive, confused, and critically ill.

**Prevalence:**
- North America: 40-60 per million population (~13,000-20,000 in the US)
- Global: 1 in 20,000 persons in Western countries; 90% of cases are autoimmune in origin

**Current monitoring gap:** Diagnosis requires a morning serum cortisol draw plus an ACTH stimulation test. Once diagnosed, patients take lifelong hydrocortisone replacement — but have no way to verify that their dosing produces a normal cortisol curve throughout the day. Dosing is adjusted based on symptoms and infrequent clinic visits, not data.

**CortiPod value:** Two distinct use cases. For **diagnosed patients** on replacement therapy, continuous monitoring could optimize the timing and dose of hydrocortisone to better mimic the natural diurnal curve — reducing both under-replacement (fatigue, crisis risk) and over-replacement (weight gain, bone loss). For **undiagnosed patients**, a persistently flat cortisol curve with no morning peak would be a strong signal to seek medical evaluation, potentially catching the disease before a crisis.

### 3. Secondary Adrenal Insufficiency (Steroid-Induced)

**What it is:** The most common form of adrenal insufficiency, and the most overlooked. When a patient takes corticosteroids (prednisone, dexamethasone, budesonide, or others) for more than a few weeks, the body's own cortisol production shuts down — the adrenal glands go dormant. When the steroids are tapered or stopped, the adrenals need 6-12 months to restart. During that window, the patient is functionally adrenal insufficient.

**Why it matters:** This is not a rare disease. Corticosteroids are prescribed for asthma, rheumatoid arthritis, lupus, inflammatory bowel disease, organ transplants, allergies, and dozens of other conditions. The recovery period is unpredictable and currently unmonitored at home.

**Prevalence:**
- North America: approximately 2.5% of the US population (~8 million people) are on chronic corticosteroids. Of those, 37-49% develop some degree of adrenal suppression — **roughly 3-4 million people** in the US alone
- Global: proportional across all developed nations where corticosteroid prescribing is widespread

**Current monitoring gap:** Recovery is monitored with periodic ACTH stimulation tests — an invasive, clinic-based procedure performed every few months during the taper. Between tests, patients and physicians are guessing. There is no home monitoring tool for adrenal recovery.

**CortiPod value:** Patients tapering off steroids could track the return of their natural cortisol production in real time. The reappearance of a morning cortisol peak and a normal diurnal decline would signal that the HPA axis is recovering, guiding taper speed — faster when recovery is strong, slower when it's not. This is the **largest single clinical population** in this document, and the unmet need is acute.

### 4. Congenital Adrenal Hyperplasia (CAH)

**What it is:** A group of inherited genetic disorders affecting cortisol synthesis in the adrenal glands. The classic form is severe and detected at birth through newborn screening. The non-classic form (NCCAH) is far more common, milder, and frequently undiagnosed — it presents later in life as early puberty, severe acne, excess body hair, irregular menstrual periods, and infertility.

**Why it matters:** Children with classic CAH require lifelong glucocorticoid replacement therapy. Dosing must be adjusted as the child grows, and both under-treatment (adrenal crisis risk, virilization) and over-treatment (growth suppression, weight gain) carry serious consequences. Non-classic CAH affects a much larger population and is frequently misdiagnosed as PCOS or idiopathic hirsutism.

**Prevalence:**
- North America: classic CAH occurs in ~1 in 15,000 births (~22,000 people living with it in the US); non-classic CAH occurs in ~1 in 200-1,000 depending on ethnicity — potentially **hundreds of thousands** undiagnosed in the US
- Global: classic ~1:15,000 births; non-classic ~1:1,000 births, with higher frequency in Ashkenazi Jewish, Hispanic, Mediterranean, and Middle Eastern populations

**Current monitoring gap:** Managed through serum 17-hydroxyprogesterone and cortisol blood draws every 3-6 months. Between visits, parents and patients rely on subjective symptom tracking to judge whether medication doses are right.

**CortiPod value:** For CAH patients on replacement therapy — especially children and adolescents during growth — continuous cortisol tracking could help parents and endocrinologists optimize dosing between clinic visits without repeated blood draws. A visible daily cortisol curve replaces guesswork with data.

### 5. Chronic Stress & Burnout

**What it is:** Not a single disease but a measurable physiological state. Prolonged activation of the HPA axis leads to cortisol dysregulation: a flattened diurnal curve (no morning peak, no evening decline), chronically elevated evening cortisol, or a blunted cortisol awakening response. This state is a documented precursor to major depression, cardiovascular disease, metabolic syndrome, type 2 diabetes, and immune dysfunction.

**Why it matters:** Chronic stress is the most prevalent health risk factor in North America, yet it has no standard objective measurement. Heart rate variability is the best available proxy, but it measures autonomic nervous system activity, not the hormonal cascade that drives long-term health consequences.

**Prevalence:**
- North America: 76% of US adults report stress affects their health (APA, 2025); 55% of the US workforce is currently experiencing burnout (Eagle Hill Consulting, 2025); 72% face moderate-to-very-high work stress (Aflac, 2025). Stress-related healthcare expenses totaled $190 billion in 2025. Chronic job stress contributes to approximately 120,000 deaths per year in the US.
- Global: WHO estimates stress-related conditions cost the global economy $1 trillion per year in lost productivity

**Current monitoring gap:** There is no standard clinical tool for measuring chronic stress objectively. HRV-based wearables provide an indirect proxy. Salivary cortisol kits exist but produce single-point measurements and are impractical for daily tracking.

**CortiPod value:** The primary consumer use case and the largest market segment. CortiPod makes stress visible and measurable in real time. Users can see whether their cortisol follows a healthy diurnal curve, track the objective impact of lifestyle interventions (sleep changes, exercise, meditation, dietary changes), and catch dysregulation before it progresses to a clinical condition.

### 6. HPA Axis Dysfunction (Cortisol Rhythm Disorders)

**What it is:** An umbrella category for conditions where the hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal feedback loop is disrupted. Cortisol is produced at the wrong times, in the wrong amounts, or fails to respond normally to stress and recovery. HPA dysfunction is documented in major depressive disorder (40-60% of patients), PTSD, generalized anxiety disorder, chronic fatigue syndrome, and anorexia nervosa.

**Why it matters:** There is strong research evidence that cortisol rhythm disruption both reflects and worsens psychiatric conditions — yet cortisol is almost never measured in routine psychiatric care. Clinicians and patients lack any objective biomarker for treatment response. Progress is assessed entirely through self-report.

**Prevalence:**
- North America: ~21 million US adults have major depression; 40-60% show measurable cortisol dysregulation — **8-13 million** with depression-related HPA dysfunction alone. PTSD affects ~13 million. Anxiety disorders affect ~40 million. Overlap is significant, but even conservatively, the population with cortisol-measurable psychiatric dysfunction is in the tens of millions.
- Global: depression affects ~280 million people worldwide (WHO); PTSD prevalence is 7-12% lifetime in most Western nations

**Current monitoring gap:** Cortisol is rarely measured in psychiatric settings despite decades of research linking it to outcomes. When measured, it is a single morning blood draw — inadequate to characterize rhythm disruption.

**CortiPod value:** A biomarker companion to mental health treatment. Tracking whether a patient's cortisol rhythm normalizes during therapy or medication adjustment could provide clinicians and patients an objective signal of biological progress — something psychiatric care almost entirely lacks today. This positions CortiPod not as a diagnostic tool but as a longitudinal wellness tracker with clinical research potential.

### 7. Shift Work & Circadian Disruption

**What it is:** Night shifts, rotating schedules, and chronic jet lag disrupt the cortisol circadian rhythm. The International Agency for Research on Cancer (IARC) classifies night shift work as a Group 2A probable carcinogen. Chronic circadian disruption is independently linked to increased risk of cardiovascular disease, type 2 diabetes, obesity, depression, and cancer.

**Why it matters:** Shift work is not a niche condition — it is a structural feature of the modern economy affecting healthcare workers, first responders, factory workers, transportation workers, and service industry employees. The health consequences are well-documented but invisible to the individuals affected.

**Prevalence:**
- North America: ~16 million Americans work non-daytime shifts (Bureau of Labor Statistics). Approximately 10-40% of shift workers develop clinically significant Shift Work Disorder.
- Global: approximately 20% of the global workforce performs shift work

**Current monitoring gap:** Sleep studies and actigraphy assess sleep quality but do not measure the hormonal consequence of circadian disruption. There is no consumer tool that shows a shift worker what their cortisol rhythm actually looks like.

**CortiPod value:** Shift workers could see exactly how their schedule affects their cortisol cycle — whether it's inverted, flattened, or chaotic — and track whether countermeasures (timed light exposure, strategic napping, meal timing, melatonin) are restoring a healthier pattern. The shift may not change, but how the body handles it can.

## "Is This You?"

> **Note:** This section is written for consumers. It uses symptom-based language and wellness framing. It does not name diseases, make diagnostic claims, or replace medical advice. Always consult a healthcare provider about your health concerns.

---

### "You've been gaining weight and no one can tell you why"

You're eating the same, exercising the same, but your body is changing. Weight accumulating around your midsection and face. Skin that bruises if you barely bump into something. Muscles that feel weak even though you haven't stopped moving. You've been to your doctor — maybe more than once. Blood work comes back "normal." You feel like something is wrong, but you can't prove it.

**What might be happening:** Your body may be producing too much of its primary stress hormone — and a single morning blood test can miss it entirely. Cortisol problems often hide behind normal-looking lab results because they depend on timing. A level that looks fine at 8 AM may be dangerously wrong at 10 PM.

**How CortiPod helps:** Track your cortisol across the full day and see the complete curve. If your levels stay elevated when they should be dropping in the evening, you'll have real data to bring to your doctor — not just symptoms they can't see. Sometimes the difference between "everything looks normal" and "let's investigate further" is the data you walk in with.

---

### "You're exhausted no matter how much you rest"

Deep, unshakeable fatigue. Low blood pressure. Craving salt. You push through the morning but crash hard by noon. Some days you can barely function, and the next day you feel almost fine. Doctors run thyroid panels and iron tests — everything comes back normal. You start to wonder if it's all in your head.

**What might be happening:** Your body's cortisol production may be lower than it should be. This is uncommon but real, and it's one of the most under-recognized patterns in medicine because the symptoms overlap with so many other things. The key clue is in the daily pattern — not just whether cortisol is present, but whether it rises and falls the way it should.

**How CortiPod helps:** A healthy cortisol curve peaks in the morning and tapers through the day. If yours is flat — low all day with no morning surge — that's a pattern worth showing your doctor. Continuous data reveals what a single blood draw at 8 AM cannot. You don't need to diagnose yourself; you need better information to share with the person who can.

---

### "You're tapering off steroids and don't know if your body is recovering"

You've been on prednisone or another corticosteroid for months — maybe for asthma, arthritis, an autoimmune condition, or a transplant. Now your doctor is reducing the dose, and you feel terrible. Crushing fatigue, joint pain, nausea, dizziness. You don't know if it's your original condition flaring up or your body struggling to restart its own cortisol production. Neither does your doctor, without expensive clinic testing.

**What might be happening:** Long-term steroid use can suppress your body's natural cortisol production. When you taper, your adrenal glands need time to wake back up — sometimes 6 to 12 months. During that window, you're in a gray zone with no way to see what's happening inside. Millions of people go through this every year.

**How CortiPod helps:** Watch your natural cortisol production return in real time. As your body recovers, you'll start to see the morning peak reappear and the normal daily rhythm take shape. Share the trend with your doctor to help guide the pace of the taper — moving faster when recovery looks strong, slowing down when it doesn't. Real data instead of waiting and hoping.

---

### "Your child's medication dose never seems quite right"

Your child takes daily medication that replaces a hormone their body doesn't make enough of on its own. Every few months, you're back at the endocrinologist for blood draws. Between visits, you're guessing. Are they getting enough? Too much? Their growth, energy, and mood seem to fluctuate, and you don't have a way to track what's happening day to day.

**What might be happening:** Children who need hormone replacement require dose adjustments as they grow. A blood draw every three months is a single snapshot — it captures one moment and misses everything in between. What happens on a Tuesday afternoon, or during a growth spurt, or when they're fighting off a cold?

**How CortiPod helps:** Continuous tracking between clinic visits gives you and your child's doctor a complete picture — not just one morning data point from a quarterly appointment. You can see whether the daily curve looks healthy, catch problems early, and flag concerns before the next visit. Less guessing, less anxiety, better conversations with the care team.

---

### "You feel like you're running on empty and burning out"

You know you're stressed. Everyone tells you to slow down. But you don't know *how* stressed your body actually is, or whether it's getting worse. You've tried meditation apps, breathwork, cold plunges, better sleep habits. Some seem to help. Some don't. You have no way to tell what's actually moving the needle and what's wishful thinking.

**What might be happening:** Chronic stress changes your cortisol pattern in ways you can't feel. Instead of a healthy peak in the morning that gives you energy and a taper through the day that lets you wind down, your curve may be flat, elevated at night, or barely rising in the morning. You can't feel this shift as it happens — it's invisible until it shows up as insomnia, unexplained weight gain, brain fog, getting sick more often, or simply the feeling that your battery never fully recharges.

**How CortiPod helps:** See your stress hormone in real numbers — not a "stress score" derived from your heart rate, but the actual hormone driving the response. Track whether your morning cortisol is healthy, whether it's dropping by evening, and whether the changes you're making to your routine are producing a measurable result. Replace guesswork with data. Know what's working. Stop doing what isn't.

### "You're working on your mental health, but can't tell if it's working"

You started therapy, or medication, or both. Your therapist asks how you're feeling. You say "a little better, maybe?" Your psychiatrist asks if the medication is helping. You're not sure — some days are better, some aren't. There's no objective signal. No blood pressure equivalent for mental health. Progress feels invisible, and doubt creeps in.

**What might be happening:** Research shows that up to 60% of people experiencing depression have a measurably disrupted cortisol rhythm — their daily curve doesn't follow the normal pattern. As effective treatment takes hold, that rhythm often begins to normalize. But today, no one is tracking it. The biological shift can precede the emotional one — your body may start recovering before you fully feel the difference.

**How CortiPod helps:** An objective biomarker alongside your subjective experience. If your cortisol curve is normalizing over weeks of treatment, that's a real, measurable signal that something is shifting biologically — even on the days when it doesn't feel like it yet. Something concrete to discuss with your therapist or psychiatrist. Not a replacement for clinical care — a companion to it.

---

### "You work nights and feel like your body never adjusts"

Night shifts, rotating schedules, red-eye flights. You sleep during the day but never feel rested. Your energy is unpredictable — wired at 3 AM, crashing at 10 AM. You've read that shift work is bad for your health, but the articles are vague and the advice is generic. You don't know what your schedule is actually doing to your body, specifically.

**What might be happening:** Your cortisol rhythm — the internal signal that tells your body when to be alert and when to rest — is likely inverted or flattened. Your body is producing the wake-up hormone when you're trying to sleep and not enough when you need to be sharp. Over months and years, this misalignment is linked to serious health consequences. But you've never been able to see it.

**How CortiPod helps:** See exactly how your schedule is affecting your cortisol cycle. Track whether strategies like timed bright light exposure, strategic napping, meal timing, or melatonin supplementation are actually helping your body adapt — or not. Your shift schedule may not change, but how your body handles it can. The first step is seeing what's happening. The second step is measuring whether your countermeasures work.

## The CortiPod Advantage

### 1. Direct measurement, not a proxy

Every wearable on the market today — Whoop, Oura, Apple Watch, Garmin — infers stress from heart rate variability. HRV is a real signal, but it's an indirect proxy that reflects autonomic nervous system activity, not the hormonal cascade that drives long-term health outcomes. CortiPod measures cortisol itself — the actual stress hormone — directly from sweat on your wrist. The difference is like estimating the weather from how people are dressed versus reading a thermometer.

### 2. A curve, not a snapshot

A single cortisol lab test is a photograph. CortiPod gives you the full movie — a reading every 15 minutes, all day, producing the continuous diurnal curve that reveals what cortisol is actually doing in your body. Cortisol disorders are pattern disorders. A normal morning level means nothing if your evening level is three times what it should be. You need the pattern to see the problem.

### 3. On your wrist, not in a lab

No blood draws. No 24-hour urine collections. No saliva kits you have to collect at specific times, mail to a lab, and wait two weeks for results. Wear CortiPod like a fitness band, open the app, see your cortisol data on your phone. Continuous monitoring that fits into your life, not the other way around.
